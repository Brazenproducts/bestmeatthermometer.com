How to Clean and Store Your Meat Thermometer Properly

Last updated: May 2026 · 5 min read

A meat thermometer goes from raw chicken to cooked steak to leftover reheating and back again. If you're not cleaning it properly between uses, you're spreading bacteria around your food — quietly, invisibly, and effectively. Here's how to do it right without damaging your thermometer.

Why Cleaning Between Uses Matters

Cross-contamination is the main risk. When you insert a thermometer into raw chicken (which can harbor Salmonella), then use it to check your pork chop without cleaning, you're transferring bacteria. The pork chop's surface may be hot enough to kill them — but if you insert the probe and pull it out before the probe itself is sanitized, that's a problem.

The FDA recommends cleaning and sanitizing thermometer probes before and after each use, and especially when moving between different proteins.

What You Can and Can't Submerge

This is the most common cleaning mistake. Most meat thermometers — including popular models like the Thermapen One — are not fully waterproof. They're splash-resistant, not dishwasher safe. The display and electronics are vulnerable to water damage if submerged.

  • The probe stem: Can be washed with soap and water, or wiped with a sanitizing wipe
  • The handle and display: Wipe only — don't submerge
  • Wired leave-in probes: The cable and probe can be washed gently; keep water away from the connector plug
  • The MEATER Plus and similar wireless probes: These are designed to be waterproof — check your manual, but most can be fully rinsed

When in doubt, check the IP (Ingress Protection) rating. IP65 or higher means it can handle rinsing. No IP rating? Wipe only.

How to Clean an Instant-Read Thermometer

  1. Fold down the probe (if it's a folding type) or leave it extended — just be careful of the sharp tip.
  2. Wipe the probe with a sanitizing wipe — food-safe alcohol wipes work great. This is the fastest option and what most food service professionals do between checks.
  3. For a deeper clean: Wash the probe stem with warm soapy water and a soft cloth or sponge. Rinse with clean water. Dry immediately.
  4. Never submerge the handle. Wipe the handle and display with a barely damp cloth if needed.
  5. Let it air dry before folding it away — moisture trapped in the storage case can cause issues over time.

How to Clean a Leave-In Probe Thermometer

  1. Disconnect the probe from the base unit before cleaning.
  2. Wash the probe and cable in warm soapy water. Use a soft cloth to wipe down the cable — don't bend the cable sharply.
  3. Rinse and dry thoroughly, especially around the cable-to-plug connection point. This area is most vulnerable to moisture damage.
  4. Wipe the base unit with a damp cloth only — don't submerge it.
  5. Store the cable loosely coiled, not tightly wound. Tight coiling damages the wire over time.

Sanitizing vs. Just Cleaning

There's a difference. Cleaning removes visible food debris. Sanitizing kills pathogens. For food safety, you want both:

  • Clean first with soap and water (sanitizer doesn't work well on organic material)
  • Then sanitize with a food-safe sanitizing wipe, a diluted bleach solution (1 tablespoon bleach per gallon of water), or 70% isopropyl alcohol
  • Air dry — don't rinse off sanitizer

For most home cooks, food-safe sanitizing wipes (available at restaurant supply stores and Amazon) are the easiest option — quick, effective, and no mixing required.

Proper Storage

How you store your thermometer affects both longevity and accuracy.

  • Use the case or sleeve it came with. The probe tip is sharp and the sensing element at the tip is delicate. Rattling around in a drawer can throw off calibration.
  • Don't store near heat sources. Drawer next to the stove, or in a case sitting on the oven — heat exposure affects battery life and can warp plastic components.
  • Store upright or horizontally in the sleeve — not with the tip pointing down unsupported.
  • Check the battery regularly. Dead batteries mid-cook are annoying. Some models have low-battery indicators; others just start giving erratic readings as the battery dies.
  • For leave-in probes: Store the cable in a loose loop, secured with a cable tie. Avoid sharp bends at the probe-cable junction.

When to Recalibrate

Cleaning and storage affect calibration over time. Test your thermometer's accuracy occasionally:

  • Ice water test: Fill a glass with ice, add water, stir. The temperature should read 32°F (0°C). If it's off by more than 2°F, recalibrate or replace.
  • Boiling water test: Boiling water at sea level is 212°F (100°C). At altitude it's lower — subtract about 1°F per 550 feet of elevation.

Some thermometers have a calibration reset button. Others require sending back to the manufacturer. Cheap thermometers often can't be recalibrated — they just need to be replaced when they drift.

Five minutes of proper care after each cook keeps your thermometer accurate, hygienic, and functional for years. It's worth it.
