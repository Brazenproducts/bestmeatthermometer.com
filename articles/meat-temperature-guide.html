Safe Cooking Temperatures: The Complete Meat Temperature Guide

Last updated: May 2026 · 5 min read

Every meat has a temperature at which it's both safe to eat and at its best. Sometimes those overlap perfectly. Sometimes there's a real gap — and navigating that gap is what separates good cooking from great cooking.

This guide covers safe minimum internal temperatures from the USDA alongside the "eating temperatures" that chefs and home cooks actually aim for. Use both to make informed decisions about your food.

Why Temperature Matters More Than Color or Time

Color is misleading. Pork can be fully cooked but still pink. Chicken breast can look white on the outside but be dangerously undercooked inside. Ground beef can turn brown before it's safe. Time estimates assume your oven temperature, cut thickness, and starting temperature are all identical to the recipe author's — they rarely are.

Internal temperature is the only objective measure of doneness. Everything else is a guess.

Beef: Steaks, Roasts, and Ground

Whole beef cuts can safely be eaten below USDA minimums because harmful bacteria live on the surface, which reaches high temperatures first. Ground beef is a different story — grinding distributes surface bacteria throughout, so it must cook all the way through.

  • Rare: 120–125°F — Cool red center, very soft
  • Medium-rare: 130–135°F — Warm red/pink center, maximum juiciness
  • Medium: 140–145°F — Pink center, firmer texture (USDA minimum for whole cuts)
  • Medium-well: 150–155°F — Slightly pink, noticeably firmer
  • Well-done: 160°F+ — No pink, fully cooked through
  • Ground beef: 160°F minimum, no exceptions
  • Beef roasts: Same scale as steaks; for low-and-slow, pull at 203–205°F for shreddable texture (brisket, chuck)

Pork: Chops, Tenderloin, and Ground

The USDA updated its pork guidance in 2011 — it's now 145°F with a 3-minute rest, not the old 160°F. Slightly pink pork at 145°F is perfectly safe and dramatically more juicy.

  • Pork chops/tenderloin: 145°F (with 3-min rest) — slightly pink, juicy
  • Pork shoulder/butt (pulled pork): 195–205°F — fully broken down, shreddable
  • Pork ribs: 190–205°F — fall-off-the-bone tender
  • Ground pork: 160°F minimum
  • Ham (pre-cooked): 140°F to reheat
  • Ham (raw/fresh): 145°F with 3-min rest

Poultry: Chicken and Turkey

Poultry is the one where most people get in trouble. Salmonella is the concern, and it requires 165°F throughout. That said, dark meat (thighs, legs) is actually better — more tender and juicy — when cooked slightly above 165°F to 175–180°F.

  • Chicken breast: 165°F — any higher and it gets dry fast
  • Chicken thigh/drumstick: 175–180°F — the connective tissue breaks down, making it more tender
  • Whole chicken: 165°F in the thickest thigh meat (away from bone)
  • Turkey breast: 165°F
  • Turkey thigh: 175°F
  • Ground chicken/turkey: 165°F
  • Stuffing (inside the bird): 165°F — it gets contaminated by raw poultry juices

Fish and Seafood

Fish overcooks incredibly fast. Many home cooks pull fish too late. Check early and often.

  • White fish (cod, halibut, tilapia): 145°F — flakes easily, just opaque
  • Salmon: 125–130°F for medium, 145°F for fully cooked
  • Tuna: 125°F for medium-rare (sushi-grade); 145°F for well done
  • Shrimp: 120°F — pink and C-shaped (J-shaped means overcooked)
  • Lobster/crab: 145°F
  • Scallops: 130°F — just opaque in center

Lamb

  • Rare: 120–125°F
  • Medium-rare: 130–135°F (most recommended for chops)
  • Medium: 140–145°F (USDA minimum for whole cuts)
  • Well done: 160°F+
  • Ground lamb: 160°F
  • Lamb shoulder (braised/slow-cooked): 190–195°F for shreddable texture

Leftovers and Reheating

When reheating cooked meat, bring it to 165°F throughout to kill any bacteria that may have grown during storage. This applies to soups, stews, and casseroles too.

A Note on Resting Time

For most meats (except poultry), resting after cooking is as important as the cooking itself. Resting allows juices to redistribute through the meat. Cut too early and those juices run all over the cutting board instead of staying in the meat.

  • Steaks and chops: 5 minutes minimum
  • Roasts: 15–20 minutes
  • Whole birds: 20–30 minutes
