How to Use a Meat Thermometer (and Never Overcook Meat Again)

Last updated: May 2026 · 5 min read

You've done everything right. Good cut of meat, seasoned properly, cooked at the right temperature — and then you slice into it and it's either raw pink or dried-out grey. A meat thermometer fixes that. It's not just a tool for nervous beginners; professional chefs use them every single time because visual cues lie.

Here's exactly how to use one.

Step 1: Know What Type of Thermometer You Have

There are two main types, and they're used slightly differently:

  • Instant-read thermometers (like the ThermoWorks Thermapen) give you a reading in 2–3 seconds. You insert them, get the number, and pull them out. Use these for checking doneness near the end of cooking.
  • Leave-in probe thermometers stay in the meat throughout cooking. They connect to a base unit or your phone via Bluetooth. Great for roasts, whole chickens, and anything going in the oven or smoker for a long stretch.

For most people, an instant-read is the workhorse. A leave-in is a nice upgrade if you do a lot of slow-roasting or smoking.

Step 2: Insert the Probe in the Right Spot

This is where most people go wrong. The probe needs to reach the coldest part of the meat — the geometric center, as far from the surface as possible.

  • Steaks and chops: Insert horizontally from the side, aiming for the center. Don't go in from the top — you'll hit the surface temperature, not the interior.
  • Whole chicken or turkey: Insert into the thickest part of the thigh, avoiding bone. Bone conducts heat differently and gives a false reading.
  • Roasts: Push the probe to the center of the thickest part, well away from bone and fat pockets.
  • Burgers: Insert from the side into the center. From the top, you only measure a thin slice of the patty.
  • Fish: Insert at the thickest point. Fish cooks fast, so check early.

Step 3: Read the Temperature Correctly

With an instant-read, wait for the reading to stabilize — usually 2–5 seconds. Most digital models beep when they've locked in. Don't pull the probe out while it's still climbing.

With a leave-in probe, set your target temperature on the unit before cooking. It'll alert you when the meat hits that temp. Resist the urge to open the oven or grill repeatedly — every peek drops the cooking environment temperature.

Step 4: Account for Carryover Cooking

This is the part recipes almost never mention: meat keeps cooking after you pull it off the heat. The residual heat in the outer layers continues moving inward for 5–10 minutes. For a thick steak, that can mean another 3–5°F of temperature rise. For a large roast, it can be 10°F or more.

Pull your meat off the heat slightly before your target temperature:

  • Steaks: Pull 3–5°F early
  • Roasts and whole birds: Pull 5–10°F early
  • Burgers and thin cuts: Carryover is minimal; pull at target

Then rest the meat loosely tented with foil. The internal temperature will coast up to where you want it.

Step 5: Know Your Target Temperatures

Here are the USDA-recommended safe temperatures plus the "chef's target" that most people actually prefer:

  • Beef steaks/roasts (medium-rare): 130–135°F (USDA minimum for whole cuts is 145°F with a 3-min rest)
  • Ground beef: 160°F — no exceptions
  • Pork: 145°F (with 3-min rest)
  • Chicken/turkey breast: 165°F
  • Chicken/turkey thigh: 175–180°F (more tender at higher temp)
  • Fish: 145°F
  • Lamb: 145°F for medium-rare

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Touching bone: Bone heats faster than meat — it'll read 10–20°F too high near a bone.
  • Checking too early then repeatedly puncturing: Each hole lets juices escape. Check once, near the end.
  • Not calibrating: Most thermometers drift over time. Test yours in boiling water (212°F at sea level) and ice water (32°F) occasionally.
  • Forgetting to clean between uses: Cross-contamination is real. Wipe the probe with a sanitizing wipe or wash with hot soapy water between readings, especially when moving from raw to checking doneness.

Quick Reference: Where to Insert by Meat Type

  • Steak → Side, through the center
  • Whole chicken → Thigh, away from bone
  • Roast → Deepest center point
  • Pork chop → Side, through center
  • Fish fillet → Thickest point, angled in
  • Burger → Side, through the patty

A good thermometer takes the guesswork out of cooking. The Thermapen One and MEATER Plus are popular choices for a reason — they're fast, accurate, and durable. But even a basic $15 digital probe beats cutting into meat to check doneness any day.

Once you start using one consistently, you'll wonder how you ever cooked without it.
