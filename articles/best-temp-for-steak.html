What Temperature is a Perfect Steak? (Rare to Well Done Guide)

Last updated: May 2026 · 5 min read

The single most common reason steaks disappoint at home is overcooked meat. It's not the cut. It's not the pan. It's cooking by time and look instead of temperature. Here's the complete breakdown of steak temperatures by doneness level — and how to hit them every time.

Steak Doneness Temperatures: The Complete Guide

Blue Rare / Extra Rare — 115–120°F

A blue steak (called "bleu" in French) is barely cooked — seared quickly on the outside, completely raw inside. The center is cold or barely warm, very red, and soft. This is a specific preference, not a beginner default. It's safe for whole-muscle cuts from quality, trusted sources. Not for everyone.

Rare — 120–125°F

Red, cool-to-warm center. Very soft, almost buttery texture. The outside has a proper sear but the interior is largely uncooked. Preferred by steak purists who want maximum flavor and tenderness in high-quality cuts. Pull from heat at 118–120°F and let carryover finish it.

Medium-Rare — 130–135°F

The sweet spot. Warm red/pink center, juicy, and tender. This is what most restaurants and chefs recommend for nearly every steak. The proteins are set enough to have good texture, but the fat is rendering properly without squeezing out the juices. If someone says "cook a steak right," they usually mean this.

Pull from heat at 127–130°F. Carryover will take it to 130–135°F during resting.

Medium — 140–145°F

Pink center, firmer texture. Still juicy but noticeably less so than medium-rare. The steak is cooked through the middle with just a hint of pink. This is the USDA's recommended minimum for whole beef cuts. A fine choice if you prefer less red meat — especially for thicker cuts where rare can feel too raw.

Pull from heat at 137–140°F.

Medium-Well — 150–155°F

Slightly pink in the very center, significantly firmer. Most of the fat has rendered out at this point. The steak will be noticeably drier than medium. Not the popular restaurant recommendation, but a legitimate preference for those who want no visible pink.

Pull from heat at 148–150°F.

Well Done — 160°F and above

No pink, fully grey-brown throughout. The steak will be firm and comparatively dry. Flavors from the Maillard crust are there, but moisture is significantly reduced. This is a valid preference — particularly for those with health concerns — but requires choosing a fattier cut (ribeye holds up much better to well-done than a lean sirloin) and considering a sauce to compensate for the dryness.

Pull from heat at 158°F.

Carryover Cooking: The Most Misunderstood Factor

When you pull a steak off high heat, it doesn't stop cooking. The hot exterior continues pushing heat toward the center for 5–10 minutes after it leaves the pan or grill. For a typical 1-inch steak, expect 3–5°F of carryover rise. For a thick 2-inch bone-in ribeye, it can be 8–10°F.

This means you should always pull the steak 3–5°F before your target temperature and let it rest.

Target temperatures to pull from heat:

  • Rare: 118°F
  • Medium-rare: 128°F
  • Medium: 138°F
  • Medium-well: 148°F
  • Well done: 158°F

How to Measure Steak Temperature Accurately

The probe placement is critical. Here's how to do it right:

  1. Insert from the side, not the top. Push horizontally into the center of the steak.
  2. Aim for the geometric center — equidistant from both flat faces and the edges.
  3. Stay away from bone in bone-in steaks. Bone reads hotter than the meat, giving false high readings.
  4. Wait for the reading to stabilize — with a fast instant-read like the Thermapen One, this takes about 2 seconds. With a slower thermometer, it may take 10 seconds.

Resting: Not Optional

Resting a steak isn't just about temperature — it's about juice distribution. When meat is hot, the muscle fibers are contracted and the juices are pushed toward the center. Resting lets the fibers relax and the juices redistribute throughout the steak. Cut too early and the juices puddle on the cutting board, not in the bite.

  • Thin steaks (under 1 inch): 3–5 minutes
  • Standard steaks (1–1.5 inch): 5–8 minutes
  • Thick steaks or bone-in (2+ inches): 8–10 minutes

Tent loosely with foil if you want, but don't wrap tightly — trapping all the steam softens the crust.

What Cut Affects What Temperature You Should Aim For

Not every steak is at its best at medium-rare. Consider:

  • Tenderloin/filet mignon: Medium-rare to medium (130–145°F). Very lean; overcooking makes it dry.
  • Ribeye: Medium-rare to medium (130–145°F). Fat marbling means it holds up slightly better at medium than a lean cut.
  • NY Strip: Medium-rare (130–135°F). Lean and firm; best at lower temps.
  • Flank and skirt steak: Medium-rare (130–135°F). These are tough cuts — always slice against the grain.
  • Hanger steak: Medium-rare to medium (130–145°F). Has great flavor but coarse texture; don't go past medium.
