Why Every Cook Needs a Meat Thermometer

There's a persistent myth that experienced cooks can tell if meat is done by pressing it, looking at the color, or watching juices. The reality is that even professional chefs use thermometers — color is an unreliable indicator of doneness, and the "poke test" is wildly inconsistent between different cuts and breeds of animal.

A meat thermometer is the single most impactful kitchen tool most people don't own. It eliminates guesswork, prevents foodborne illness, and — just as importantly — stops you from overcooking perfectly good steaks. Serving a steak cooked to 150°F because you were nervous it wasn't done is as much of a failure as undercooking it.

The USDA sets minimum safe internal temperatures for a reason: harmful bacteria like Salmonella, E. coli O157:H7, and Listeria are killed at specific temperatures. Chicken must reach 165°F. Ground beef needs to hit 160°F. Whole pork can be served at 145°F — a rule that changed in 2011, allowing for juicy, slightly pink pork for the first time. A thermometer is the only way to know you've hit those numbers.

The good news? A quality instant-read thermometer costs $15–$20. That's less than a single steak. There's genuinely no reason not to own one.
